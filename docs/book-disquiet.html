Notes on The Book of Disquiet

Jason Cairns

2022-05-04

Pessoa/Soares verges on solipsism throughout the book. Maybe he believes in the exterior world. It doesn’t “matter” to him, however, as the world out there still needs to find it’s way in to his mind in order to be an object of consideration. There it must compete with other purely imaginary objects, which are, to him, vastly superior in the limitless possibilities of their existence.

The self-awareness of such awareness is itself a significant object of consideration to him. Consciousness in particular is treated as a unique affliction besetting him among a few literary others, with the masses wallowing in waking unconsciousness. The affliction is such, due to the entirely distinct pain of awareness. At the very least it gets in the way of higher forms of unconsciousness, such as dreaming.

The real world is nothing but the tedium of endless rain. The mind is where infinite plains can be roamed.

The irony of his exultation of the wild imaginary is that his own book contains not a single fantastical element. If anything, he outlines a reality that is seldom described in such detail - the soul. His soul, in particular. Of course, it is illustrated by way of heteronym, but with his own experience being singular, such illustration draws entirely from himself. Through his rich description of his soul he breathes life into it. While in denial about the death of a loved one I find myself extending such denial to a long-dead author whom I have never met. If abstract mathematical objects are to exist independently of mind, mind seems equally capable of existing in it’s own right. His description is a poetic equivalent of a Euclidean definition. Consideration of such points to something that remains true irrespective of anything, including whether they have ever existed.
